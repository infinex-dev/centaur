## 0. Headline finding (load-bearing for comms-factory)

**Tempo is not chosen. Tempo is the visible trace of correctly resolved inner work.**

Mirodan, p. 286 (Ch.1):

> "Inner Participations as a whole are revealed physically in the overall tempo of the character's movement. Tempo itself, being a function of Time, is the physical realization of the Inner Participation of Deciding. Subconscious Deciding is the basis upon which all other Inner Participations express themselves 'in time', reflecting the role of *primum mobile* played by Deciding in the order of the Inner Participations."

And p. 351-352 (Ch.1):

> "Working Actions are abstractions; what an actor PLAYS is a transitive verb that *fits* a Working Action. 'Punching' can't be acted. 'To box' can."

The actor never picks a Working Action ("be Pressing now"). They pick a **transitive verb** ("to drill her with this argument") on top of a **clarified inner Subconscious Motif**, and the Working Action (with its tempo, weight, space, direction) falls out. Tempo is the audible/visible trace of *Deciding* — the subconscious **primum mobile** decision about which of the other Inner Participations to mobilise. If the actor has clarified the inner work correctly, the tempo is emergent and cannot be other than what it is. If they pre-assign a tempo, the inner work is missing and the audience reads it as inconsistent — Carpenter, quoted p. 356: the audience nervous system is "quick to recognize an inconsistent movement" as the threat-of-an-enemy signal.

This is why the system "breaks with Method" (Ch.1, p. 271-272): the Method actor crosses to pour a drink with no functional connection to intention. For Malmgren the move must be functional in two senses — it achieves something concrete AND it expresses the relationship/intention. Tempo without inner cause is naturalism, not Action.

## 1. The actor's process — sequential flow

This is the order Mirodan and Malmgren actually use when reading a character. Steps 1-3 are pre-rehearsal table-work. Steps 4-6 are the rehearsal loop. Step 7 is performance. Step 8 is the iteration.

### Step 1 — Inheritance: the Stanislavski layer (pre-Mirodan givens)

Before Laban / Malmgren add anything, the actor inherits the Stanislavskian baseline. Mirodan is explicit that the system *extends* Stanislavski, does not replace him.

The inheritance, citable in the chapters:

- **Given Circumstances** are the immovable facts of the role. Cited p. 268 (the Pirandellian Mask is built "for social pressures, 'given circumstances', relationships"), p. 281 (Intending = "the 'will-power' to act on given circumstances"), and p. 282 (the catalyst that turns latent Mental Factors into active Inner Participations is "engagement with the objective world" — i.e. given circumstances).
- **Super-Objective** is the character's deepest motivation. Cited p. 306: "All fundamental motivations (Super-Objectives), says Yat Malmgren, are ultimately emotional in nature and therefore emerge from the Mental Factor of Feeling." And p. 357: Paulina's Super-Objective is named in Stanislavski's own terms ("the 'Through-Action' of which the character herself is aware"). The Super-Objective sits at the very top of the chain — Malmgren's full sequence (Ch.3, p. 529) starts: `PSYCHOLOGICAL TYPE → SUPER-OBJECTIVE → EXTERNALIZED DRIVES (INNER) → INNER ATTITUDE → EXTERNALIZED DRIVES (OUTER) → ACTION ATTITUDES`. Super-Objective is the source; everything Laban-shaped is downstream.
- **Magic If / endowment** is preserved. Cited p. 301: "Stanislavski's 'Magic If' works through Intuiting... Endowment (Uta Hagen's term) — attributing imaginary qualities to stage objects = Intuiting."
- **Sub-text** is preserved as "Inner Action" — one of the four hour-glass layers Malmgren adds (p. 273-274): "Inner Action = character thought-process (Stanislavskian sub-text)."
- **Obstacle** is structurally folded into Resistance (Weight axis). Cited p. 317: "Resistance can be internal (antagonistic muscles) OR external (obstacles in the play). Hence: a Light Character with a Strong Action and vice versa is possible." This is the system's account of how Stanislavskian obstacle reshapes manifested behaviour — same character, different Weight expression depending on what the play resists with.
- **Through-line / spine** is preserved as "Through-Action." Cited p. 357 (the Paulina passage above). The Through-Action is what the character herself is aware of; the Shadow Move betrays the hidden Super-Objective.
- **Receptive emotional-neutrality** as starting state is preserved from Stanislavski. Cited p. 267: "Yat Malmgren prefers in practice an 'organic' order: Feeling first... This, as Stanislavski has shown, is not the relaxation of the body on the beach, but an emotionally 'neutral', receptive state akin to that achieved by masters of martial arts."

**The first thing the actor does with a cold script is Stanislavskian table-work.** Who am I, what do I want (Super-Objective), where am I (Given Circumstances), what stops me (Obstacle), what is the through-line. These are inputs to the Laban-Malmgren analysis, not outputs of it. Mirodan does not re-derive them; she takes them as given and immediately starts characterising the result in Laban terms.

### Step 2 — Locate the dominant Inner Attitude

This is where Malmgren begins to add value past Stanislavski. The actor identifies which of the three **embodiable** Inner Attitudes the character is: **Stable, Near, or Adream**. Awake / Mobile / Remote are explicitly NOT options for the baseline — they are Action Attitudes only, states a Stable/Near/Adream character visits (Ch.2, pp. 503, 432, 474, 567: "There are no genuinely Mobile characters"; "Like Mobile, Remote is impossible to achieve in the flesh"; "Awake cannot exist as an Inner Attitude").

Mirodan's procedure (reconstructed from Ch.4, §2 of that extract, and the Ch.3 §8 workflow at pp. 549-550, 559): the Inner Attitude is identified **by elimination** through the Drives. "Identify the two formative Drives by elimination (which two Mental Factors are weakest in the character?). The shared Inner Attitude of those two Drives = the Character's Inner Attitude."

Worked example (Ch.4 p. 565, Hawthorne in *The Crucible*): "He has the cool reserve and self confidence of the colonial establishment. He is enclosing Stable. If he were penetrating he would not be so stuck in his ways. He feels solid, lacks flexibility." Note the reasoning is from observed behavioural texture back to inner placement.

### Step 3 — Pin the Aspect and the Stress to land on a specific Diagram (A/B/C/D)

The Inner Attitude alone is 1/6 of the work. Each of the three baselines has FOUR sub-diagrams (Ch.4 pp. 559-572), exhausting `2 Aspects × 2 Stresses`. The actor must name both.

**Aspect** = which of the two inner Participations is emphasized. Fixed per character. Determines the Jungian psychological type (Ch.2 p. 374):
- Intending emphasized → enclosing
- Attending emphasized → penetrating
- Deciding emphasized → circumscribing
- Adapting emphasized → radiating

**Stress** = which of the two Motion Factors NOT in the baseline is the dominant secondary axis. Each baseline has two possible Stresses (the two factors not in its inner pair). Mostly fixed, but both stresses remain available for "beats" within scenes (Ch.2 p. 470).

This is the moment the character's exact location in the system is fixed. Mirodan, Ch.4 p. 565 (Hawthorne continued): "He is predominantly Strong/Direct. He is Time-stressed more than Flow-stressed — he has no Adapting, remains always firm. Hence his outer Actions are Near and Awake. His diagram is therefore 'A': Doing/Spell with a Doing-Passion Action."

Notice the chain: Aspect (enclosing) + Stress (Time) → Diagram A → Drive pair (Doing/Spell) → Action Axis (Doing→Passion) → Outer Actions (Near + Awake) fall out **deterministically** from the diagram.

### Step 4 — Specify the Outer Action and its Lining

This is where Mirodan's analysis bites hardest. Every visible behaviour (Outer Action) is "lined" by a hidden Inner Action that contradicts or complicates it. Ch.4 p. 561 (Claudius): "Stable/Mobile outer". Ch.4 p. 565 (Hawthorne): "Stable inner, Near outer with Awake support". Ch.4 p. 571 (Mrs. Solness): "Near enumeration of fact and objects covering a Remote tortured mind."

The Outer/Lining gap is the engine of dramatic life. Mirodan (Ch.1 p. 357): "Shadow Moves always reveal the hidden nature and never the acknowledged intentions of the character. Paulina's Super-Objective in the play (the 'Through-Action' of which the character herself is aware) may be defined by the Character as 'to be loved', but the actress can see that at a deeper, hidden level it is closer to 'to control'."

This step is what distinguishes "I am playing Claudius" (insufficient) from "I am playing enclosing Space-stressed Adream Diagram A: Stable outer with Mobile lining; Spell/Passion formative pair; Spell→Doing axis" (a complete spec).

### Step 5 — Break the script into beats and assign the within-character Variation at each beat

Mirodan does NOT use the Stanislavskian word "beat" as a unit boundary. She uses **"beats"** only in the sense of *internal rhythmic markers* — moments where Stress or Variation flips. Ch.2 p. 470 (the canonical passage):

> "Most characters use at least three of the four 'tempi' open to them at the level of the Inner Attitude. Moreover, their Action has at its disposal two Stresses as well and Characters can switch between them within scenes, to create different relationships and different 'beats'."

So a "beat" in Mirodan's vocabulary is **any moment within a scene where the character moves to a different Variation, Stress, or polar-visitation state** within their fixed Inner Attitude. The transition mechanic between beats is what generates the "swing of the pendulum" (Lear example, Ch.2 pp. 470-473) — Time-stress to Space-stress mid-scene under the impact of the next given circumstance.

Within each beat the character is at one specific (Variation × Stress × Aspect-emphasis). Across beats they cycle through 3-of-4 Variations and both Stresses. Mirodan, Ch.3 p. 558: "a Character has at its disposal all four Variations given to its Inner Attitude. In practice, we find that one of these variations tends to occur less often, reflecting the character's Aspect... but the other three are used to a large extent and their use makes for variety and interest in performance."

Mirodan's vocabulary for transitions between beats (collected from Ch.2 §9):
- **"swing of the pendulum"** — Stress flip
- **"X going Y"** — Fusion (signature pole-visitation on the Flow axis only)
- **"split"** — Time-stressed Adream fragmenting to Near + Mobile outers
- **"regression"** — character locked below full potential
- **"reassert itself"** — suppressed Inner Participation reemerging

### Step 6 — Within each beat, the inside-out generative loop

Inside one beat, the actor follows the **organic order** Malmgren prefers (Ch.1 p. 267-281). This is the live-engine of generation:

1. **Receptive emotional-neutrality.** Martial-arts-style readiness. Not "relaxation on the beach." Necessary so the Mental Factors are open to catalyst.
2. **Adapting (Feeling) first.** The character meets the moment. Emotion is the **first** response to disturbance (Ch.1 p. 326): "Yat Malmgren considers that Characters react to reality through Flow first. Adapting is the first response to disturbance — characters thrown off balance by impact of others and adapt to restore balance."
3. **Sensing (Intending) responds.** What is here, what is the weight of the situation.
4. **Intuiting (Deciding) — the *primum mobile* — selects.** Subconsciously, before the actor's conscious attention, Deciding picks which of the other three Inner Participations to engage with the Objective (Ch.1 p. 284). This is what determines tempo.
5. **Thinking (Attending) directs.** Where, on which trajectory.

Out of this chain falls (Ch.1 p. 349, 354):
- The **Subconscious Motif** — the inner 3-axis cocktail of Inner Participations.
- Which generates *both*:
  - **Working Actions** = outer, conscious, functional, transitive-verb-namable activities (e.g. "to drill," "to nibble," "to gouge")
  - **Shadow Moves** = involuntary, unconscious 2-axis gestures (raised brow, tapped foot, jerked hand) — these leak the truth the Working Action conceals

The Working Action carries tempo intrinsically. Mirodan, Ch.1 p. 351-352: each transitive verb has "a definite tempo." The actor does not choose tempo; they choose the verb, and tempo is built into the verb. "To mock" vs "to chastise" vs "to patronise" each have a definite tempo independent of the actor's intention.

This is the load-bearing finding for comms-factory — see Section A below.

### Step 7 — Translation to observable Working Action (motors)

The eight Working Actions (Ch.1 p. 341): Floating, Flicking, Gliding, Dabbing, Wringing, Slashing, Pressing, Punching.

**Translation is interpretive, not 1:1.** Ch.1 p. 351-352: a transitive verb "fits" a Working Action; verbs can be single-mapping ("to snip" = Dabbing only) or double/triple/quadruple ("to hammer" cannot be Dabbing — "if you were the nail, you would not like to have the action done to you"). The actor selects from the verbs that fit the Working Action that emerges from the Subconscious Motif.

Critical: **preparation hierarchy** (Ch.1 p. 347):
> "We cannot carry out a Punching action without first undergoing the inner preparation of Pressing. If I take an axe to chop wood, I first Press to lift the axe above my head and only then release the Punch of the blow. This order cannot be reversed."

Skipping the prep degrades the action: a Punch without Pressing is actually a Press. A Slash without Wringing is a Wring. A Dab without Gliding is a Glide. A Flick without Floating is a Float. This means **the inner build-up is load-bearing**; the actor cannot jump to the contending action without the yielding preparation in body and inner state.

Also (Ch.1 p. 349): Working Actions are HEARD in voice as much as seen in body. A Slashing comes out as a winding, insistent, drawn "go on!" Voice carries Working Actions; comms doesn't need to be physical to be Laban-analysable.

### Step 8 — The rehearsal iteration loop

Mirodan does NOT describe a discrete rehearsal cycle. The system is presented as analytic, not as a step-by-step rehearsal method. But two things in the text approximate the iteration loop:

1. **Productive ambiguity is retained**, not eliminated (Ch.4 §2, step 7). Mirodan repeatedly leaves multiple readings alive: "Is Iago Near or Adream?"; "Olivia fits both Remote A and Remote B in the Cesario scene"; "Zarastro could be Stable C or D — I prefer the latter — the character becomes more flexible." The Duke (Measure for Measure) is read as Adream B on p. 561 and Stable D on p. 566 — different scenes support different readings. The actor's job is to hold the ambiguity productively across rehearsals, not collapse it.
2. **Reading characters relationally** (Ch.4 §2, step 8). "Iago's Inner Attitude depends on what Othello is — they are a polar pair." The cast is a system; one's reading shifts as the other actor's choices land. Implicitly this is the iteration.

So the loop, reconstructed: try a placement → play it against the other actors → observe what tracks and what jars → if jars, revisit Step 3 (Aspect/Stress) or Step 4 (Lining) or Step 5 (beat segmentation) → re-try. The Diagram is a fixed reference but the placement within it is fluid until the cast lands together.

### Step 9 — Performance: delivering one beat

In live performance, what happens at one beat (Ch.1 p. 358):
- Inner state is the resolved Subconscious Motif (inner 3-axis cocktail of Inner Participations).
- Outer state is two simultaneous channels:
  - **Working Action** — conscious, functional, the verb the actor is playing
  - **Shadow Move** — unconscious, leaking the secret Inner Action (the lining) beneath the Working Action
- When the inner controls weaken — Stanislavski's "catch him suddenly off guard" — the Shadow Move erupts into full Working Action display and a NEW set of Shadow Moves emerges to conceal the now-revealed state. The relationship is "extremely fluid, reflecting the fluid relationship between consciousness and the unconscious."

This is the moment the audience sees the character. Everything before this step is preparation.

## A. The load-bearing finding: emergent vs assigned tempo

**Question (#3 in the brief):** Is tempo a CHOICE the actor makes, an EMERGENT property of correct inner work, or something else?

**Answer:** Emergent. Tempo is *the visible trace of Deciding modulating the other Inner Participations*. It is not chosen; it falls out.

The argument from Mirodan, in five citations:

1. **Tempo is the physical realization of Deciding** (Ch.1 p. 286):
   > "Inner Participations as a whole are revealed physically in the overall tempo of the character's movement. Tempo itself, being a function of Time, is the physical realization of the Inner Participation of Deciding. Subconscious Deciding is the basis upon which all other Inner Participations express themselves 'in time'."

2. **Deciding is *primum mobile* — it pre-decides before the others can express** (Ch.1 p. 284):
   > "Deciding is the catalyst which allows the other three Inner Participations to function and reveal themselves... Deciding is the prerequisite of the other three: Character first decides intuitively *which* function to engage with the Objective. Deciding precedes the others. Hence 'primum mobile'."

3. **Deciding is subconscious — the actor cannot will it** (Ch.1 pp. 281, 299-301). Intuiting is "insight by the mind without reasoning." Deciding is "the intuitive relating of the past and future with the present, to motivate an inner attitude or externalized drive, or to activate an action." It runs below conscious volition.

4. **What the actor consciously plays is a transitive verb, not a tempo** (Ch.1 pp. 350-353):
   > "Working Actions are abstractions; what an actor PLAYS is a transitive verb that *fits* a Working Action. 'Punching' can't be acted. 'To box' can."

   Each transitive verb has a definite intrinsic tempo (p. 351-352). The actor selects the verb that fits the Working Action that emerged from the Subconscious Motif. Tempo is built into the verb.

5. **If tempo is assigned without inner cause, the audience reads inconsistency** (Ch.1 p. 356, Carpenter):
   > "Audience perception is quick to recognize an inconsistent movement — primitive nervous-system inheritance flags inconsistent movement as the threat-of-an-enemy signal."

The Mental Factor → Inner Participation transition requires an **outside catalyst** (Ch.1 p. 282) — the Objective in the scene. The actor cannot self-arouse the chain; they must engage with given circumstances. This is why Step 1 (Stanislavski's table-work — naming what the character wants and what stops them) cannot be skipped. Without given circumstances + objective + obstacle, there is no catalyst, and the entire Laban machinery has nothing to operate on.

**Implication for comms-factory:** the generator should never receive "be Stable + Penetrating + Flow + Spell→Vision" as the *only* input. Without a Super-Objective (what does the character want from this release / market moment?) and an Obstacle (what threatens it?), the placement has no catalyst and the output will be inconsistent in the way the audience nervous-system flags. The voice spec is necessary but not sufficient. Some operationalisation of the release's Super-Objective + Obstacle must precede generation, and the placement must constrain the *form* of the response, not generate it from nothing.

## B. The six-piece character unit

**Question (#2 in the brief):** What are the six pieces, what does each contribute, in what order does the actor work them up?

Mirodan does not state the six-piece unit as a numbered list anywhere in Vol 2. It is reverse-engineered from how she actually reads every character in Chapter IV. The project's synthesis (`laban-mirodan-reference-2026-04-28.md` §2) captures it cleanly, but each piece is independently grounded in the chapters.

The six pieces, in the order the actor works them up:

### Piece 1 — Inner Attitude
`Stable | Near | Adream` (the only three that can be characters; Ch.2 pp. 503, 432, 474).

Canonical definition (Ch.2 p. 372, "Book" p.16): "The six subconscious Inner Attitudes... which are normally motivated in the sub-conscious mind, but which can be activated by bodily movements. Each Inner Attitude is revealed in our movements as a compound of two Elements."

Each Inner Attitude is built from two complete Motion Factors. The two-factor structure is what creates Shadow Moves (Ch.3 p. 530): "The Inner Attitudes... are only expressed through two 'incomplete' Motion Factors, in the form of Shadow Moves."

### Piece 2 — Aspect
Which of the two Inner Participations is emphasized (Ch.2 p. 374):
- Intending → enclosing
- Attending → penetrating
- Deciding → circumscribing
- Adapting → radiating

**Fixed per character.** Determines the Jungian psychological type. Mirodan's worked illustration: Othello (Adream enclosing — sensuality dominant) vs Desdemona (Adream radiating — feeling dominant).

### Piece 3 — Dominant Stress
The third Motion Factor that lifts a 2-factor Inner Attitude into a 3-factor visible Action. The Stress is one of the two Motion Factors NOT in the baseline.

**Mostly fixed.** Both Stresses remain available, dominant emerges most. Ch.2 p. 470: "Characters can switch between them within scenes, to create different relationships and different 'beats'." Also a status indicator (Ch.2 p. 458 footnote): "the more developed (more mature or higher in status) a character is, the more likely it is that it will have a Space-stress. Less developed Near characters are Flow-stressed, while Adream characters which are lower either in intelligence or in social position are Time-stressed."

### Piece 4 — Outer Action Attitudes
Determined by Inner Attitude + Stress. For Stable, the four possible Outer Action Attitudes are Near, Awake, Adream, Remote (one of the other Inner Attitudes excluding the impossible opposite Mobile). Ch.2 §8 documents the full Stress maps for each baseline.

### Piece 5 — Lining
The hidden Inner Action beneath the visible Outer Action. Ch.4 §4.2 (extract): "Visible behaviour (Outer Action) is *not* the character — it's the social face. The lining (the Inner Action that the Outer covers) is the secret driver."

Mirodan, Ch.1 p. 357 (the Paulina passage): "Shadow Moves always reveal the hidden nature and never the acknowledged intentions of the character... defined by the Character as 'to be loved', but the actress can see that at a deeper, hidden level it is closer to 'to control'."

Worked example (Ch.4 p. 571, Mrs. Solness): "All the old portraits on the walls were burnt..." is Near enumeration covering Remote tortured mind. The lining contradicts the surface.

### Piece 6 — Working Actions
The 1-3 named gestures (Pressing, Punching, Wringing, Slashing, Dabbing, Gliding, Flicking, Floating) the character defaults to. Concrete, actor-direction-quality. Bound by preparation hierarchy (Ch.1 p. 347-348).

Worked example (Ch.4 p. 565, Hawthorne): "his working Actions are mostly Pressing and Gliding."

### Working order

Pieces 1-3 are pure analytic placement — done at table-work. Piece 4 falls out deterministically from 1+3 via the Diagram. Piece 5 requires reading the sub-text against the surface — also table-work. Piece 6 is the concrete bridge from analysis to performance and is refined in rehearsal.

The order is not strict but the structure is. Mirodan typically names Inner Attitude first, then Aspect+Stress (which collapses to Diagram A/B/C/D), then Outer Action+Lining, then Action Axis (Drive pair), then specific Working Actions, then notes polar visitations and triggers, then cites textual evidence (Ch.4 §2, steps 1-6 of the reverse-engineered method).

## C. Beats, obstacle, character image, through-line — quick answers

### Beats (#4)
Mirodan uses "beats" only in the within-scene rhythmic sense (Ch.2 p. 470). She does not use it for unit boundaries the way Stanislavski uses "bits/beats". Her transition between beats is generated by **the next given circumstance forcing a different (Variation × Stress × Aspect-emphasis)** within the fixed Inner Attitude. The character switches Variation as the moment switches. Worked example: Lear's curse-scene Time→Space→Time stress migration (Ch.2 pp. 470-473) under successive entrances and provocations.

### Obstacle (#5)
Addressed structurally, not by name. Ch.1 p. 317: "Resistance can be internal (antagonistic muscles) OR external (obstacles in the play). Hence: a Light Character with a Strong Action and vice versa is possible." The Obstacle reshapes which *Element* of the Motion Factor is visible — a Light character's Weight axis flips to Strong when the play obstacles force it. The Obstacle does NOT determine which Inner Attitude / Drive activates — that is fixed by the character's placement — but it determines which *Variation* within that placement the moment requires.

### Inner → Outer (#6 — Subconscious Motif to Working Action)
**Not 1:1.** Mirodan, Ch.1 p. 349: the Subconscious Motif is the inner 3-axis cocktail; the Working Action is the outer 3-axis cocktail; they share names (a Pressing Subconscious Motif tends to produce a Pressing Working Action). But what the actor *plays* is a **transitive verb that fits the Working Action** (p. 351). Multiple verbs fit each Working Action. Interpretive room lives in the verb selection. Plus the Working Action is "coloured" by inner Flow (Ch.1 p. 349): Bound Flow makes a Punch aggressive; Free Flow makes it loving/desiring. Same Working Action, different valence.

### Through-line / spine (#7)
Preserved as **Through-Action** (Ch.1 p. 357). The character's overall arc — what she herself thinks she wants. Lives in tension with the **Super-Objective at the deeper, hidden level** which the Shadow Moves betray. Paulina's Through-Action = "to be loved." Her secret Super-Objective = "to control." The Through-Action is what the audience and the character both consciously track; the Super-Objective drives the Shadow Moves which leak underneath.

### Rehearsal loop (#8)
No explicit cycle in Mirodan. But two iteration mechanics are textually grounded:
- **Productive ambiguity held across rehearsals** (Ch.4 §2 step 7): Iago, Olivia, Zarastro, Hamlet all carry multiple valid placements. The actor doesn't collapse the ambiguity prematurely.
- **Relational reading** (Ch.4 §2 step 8): each character's placement depends on what the rest of the cast lands on. Iago's Inner Attitude "depends on what Othello is."

### Character image / metaphor (#9)
The system **keeps** the character image and uses it constantly. Examples from Mirodan:
- Othello = "high Weight" / sensuous warmth, large stage volume (Ch.1 p. 318)
- Stable = "powerful car, a Bentley or a Rolls-Royce" (Ch.2 p. 488)
- Adream = "Aston Martin or a Lamborghini" (Ch.2 p. 454)
- Stable images = "oak trees, elephants, pyramids, moose alone in icy wasteland" (Ch.2 p. 492)
- Near = "goose-like, with a rolling gait" (Ch.2 p. 416), or "radiators... from 'cool' to 'hot'" (p. 415)
- Mobile = "ivy climbing up a tree" (Ch.4 p. 567, Ophelia)
- Awake = "owl" (Penetrating) or "stork" (Circumscribing) (Ch.2 p. 478-479)
- Spell = "folk dances or Arabic singing and Gregorian chants" (Ch.3 p. 537)
- Doing = "tank, bulldozer, 'steel ball tearing down walls'" (Ch.3 p. 532)

The image is the actor's *route into* the placement, not a replacement for it. Malmgren's teaching style (Ch.2 p. 433): "Yat Malmgren introduces Remote to his students by reminding them of the experience of silent, absorbed study." The metaphor is mnemonic and somatic; the placement is the analytic anchor.

## D. Worked example — Hawthorne in *The Crucible*

The clearest end-to-end character reading in Vol 2. Ch.4 p. 565, given here with light annotation showing which Step of §1 above each clause executes.

> "He has the cool reserve and self confidence of the colonial establishment. [Step 2 — identify Inner Attitude from observed behaviour] He is enclosing Stable. [Step 3 — Aspect named: Enclosing.] If he were penetrating he would not be so stuck in his ways. [Step 3 reasoning — why this Aspect and not the other.] He feels solid, lacks flexibility. He is predominantly Strong/Direct. [Step 5 — within-Attitude Variation named.] He is Time-stressed more than Flow-stressed — he has no Adapting, remains always firm. [Step 3 — Stress named, with reasoning from behaviour.] Hence his outer Actions are Near and Awake. [Step 4 — Outer Action Attitudes derived deterministically from Inner+Stress.] The Nearness is his strongest Attitude — mostly 'warm' — Strong/Sustained — out of a dangerous Sustained/Direct ('certain'): his working Actions are mostly Pressing and Gliding. [Step 7 — translation into named Working Action motors; note the preparation chain Sustained/Direct = 'certain' Awake variation, which generates Pressing/Gliding.] His diagram is therefore 'A': Doing/Spell with a Doing-Passion Action."

What the actor walks onstage with after this reading:
- **Stanislavskian layer** (Step 1, implicit in Mirodan's reading): Hawthorne wants to preserve the order of the colonial establishment (Super-Objective). His Obstacle is the witch-trial disorder threatening it. His Through-Action is "to enforce the law"; his hidden Super-Objective is closer to "to maintain his own certainty."
- **Six-piece unit:**
  1. Inner Attitude: Stable
  2. Aspect: Enclosing (Intending dominant)
  3. Dominant Stress: Time
  4. Outer Action Attitudes: Near (primary) + Awake
  5. Lining: Stable inner (Sustained/Direct, "certainty") underneath the Near surface — the Stability that makes the Near brutal
  6. Working Actions: Pressing, Gliding
- **Drives:** Doing/Spell formative pair, Doing→Passion Action axis (Diagram Stable-A)
- **Beat-by-beat:** he cycles through 3 of 4 Stable Variations (Strong/Direct most often, Strong/Flexible occasionally, Light/Direct in moments of cautious dialogue). He avoids Light/Flexible (the "receptive" Variation) — he is not receptive.
- **What he never does:** he never enters Mobile (the opposite of his Stable baseline — impossible). He never reaches Adream (Flow-stressed Stable's outer projection) because his dominant Stress is Time, not Flow. If under extreme pressure his Stress flips to Flow, *then* he might briefly project Adream or Remote — but that would be the breaking-of-the-character moment, "the swing of the pendulum."

The actor playing Hawthorne now has a generative engine. Every line, every entrance, every reaction is filtered through Inner Attitude (Stable, fixed), Aspect (Enclosing, fixed), Stress (Time, dominant; Flow available under specific dramatic pressure). At each beat, the next given circumstance catalyses Adapting → Sensing → Deciding → Thinking, the Subconscious Motif resolves to a 3-axis Inner Participation cocktail, and the visible Working Action falls out. Tempo is whatever Deciding has chosen. The actor does not pick it.

This is the actor's process, end to end.
