## Visual fingerprint (1 tight paragraph)

Polymarket's visual language is austere financial-terminal cosplay dressed in a single saturated blue. The atomic unit of comms is not a designed asset — it is a screenshot of the live market: white card, Inter, a black-and-blue line chart, a percentage in bold, a market question as a wry deadpan sentence. Their "brand" is the product UI exported as imagery, and they have leaned hard into that — a Substack embed partnership ships the same card as a live widget, ICE/NYSE-deal posture frames them as grown-up market infrastructure, and "Against all odds" framed the U.S. relaunch as victory-lap-not-marketing. The register is news-anchored, dry, slightly cocky, and the visual reads more Bloomberg-terminal-meets-Cabel-Sasser than crypto-pump. No mascots, no gradients, no illustration system worth speaking of. The chart IS the illustration.

## Motion vocabulary

- **Transitions** — Inferred: hard cuts between market-card frames, with the percentage value as the persistent visual anchor. The Substack-embed live widget shows the design instinct: data ticks update in place rather than slide-in. When motion is used in promo (election-cycle Meta ads, US-app relaunch), it appears to be screen-capture-style with cursor and tap interactions, not bespoke animation. Live broadcast integrations (UFC "fan prediction scoreboard") move odds in real time as the event progresses — odds-as-heartbeat, not odds-as-reveal.
- **Element motion** — The chart line drawing in left-to-right is the signature gesture. Percentage numerics count up/down. The candidate/event headshot or icon is static; only the number breathes. No bouncing, no kinetic typography, no easing acrobatics.
- **Tempo** — Newsroom-pace. A Polymarket video clip behaves more like a CNBC L3 lower-third than a TikTok hook. Cuts on news beats, not on music beats. The 60 Minutes / CNBC Squawk Box appearances of Coplan visually anchor the brand to broadcast-business-news pacing.
- **Sound role** — Inferred: silent-first design. The chart-screenshot meme works without audio (it gets retweeted, screenshotted again, dropped into other people's posts). When sound exists in app-walkthrough ads, it's UI taps + minimal score. No drops, no risers, no crypto-influencer voiceover energy.

## Type & color discipline

- **Typefaces** — Inter, full stop. Confirmed on polymarket.com/brand. Weights provided: Regular, Medium, Semibold, Bold. No serif companion in product; the Substack/Oracle newsletter does pick up a serif headline (Substack default), which is the one place a second typeface leaks in.
- **Type personality** — Functional and unadorned. Inter is doing financial-UI work, not voice work. Hierarchy is built with weight + scale, never with face-mixing or display fonts. Numbers are the heroes — percentages set big, semibold/bold, with the market question set smaller in regular weight. The voice in headlines is the opposite of the typography's voice: dry-witty news-desk sentences sitting inside neutral-corporate type.
- **Palette** — Three colors. Poly Blue **#2E5CFF** (RGB 46 92 255), Black **#000000**, White **#FFFFFF**. Plus state colors borrowed from finance: green for YES/up, red for NO/down. No accent palette, no tints, no gradients in the brand kit. (Brandfetch corroborates.)
- **Color discipline** — Brand guide says explicitly: "Monochrome usage is preferred… primary blue should be used for key actions and brand moments." Read that as: blue is rationed. The platform itself is overwhelmingly black-on-white with blue used sparingly for interactive affordance. This is the single most distinctive thing about Polymarket against the rest of crypto — they refuse the purple/teal/neon gradient default. They look like an exchange, not a memecoin.

## 3 visual anti-patterns

1. **Polymarket does NOT do illustration.** No editorial illustration, no character mascot, no hand-drawn anything. The blog has minimal hero imagery. Their visual self-image is "data, presented." Trying to make Polymarket-style work that leans on cute illos would betray the brand's chosen austerity.
2. **Polymarket does NOT do gradients, glow, or neon.** This is the loudest negation against the rest of crypto. No purple-to-teal, no holographic, no Solana-style chroma. One blue, used sparingly, on white. Anything iridescent reads as a different category.
3. **Polymarket does NOT do crypto-influencer kinetic edit.** No whip-pans, no "BREAKING:" red-banner shake, no Andrew Tate jumpcut. Their tempo is broadcast-news / Squawk-Box, not TikTok-trader. Even the election-cycle Meta ads were straight info-card style, not hype-edit.

## Steal vs. avoid (for Rambo)

The ONE technique worth porting: **make the live data card the hero, not a decoration**. Polymarket's whole comms machine works because the artifact (chart + percent + sentence + thumbnail) is functional, native to the product, and embed-distributable. A Remotion template that takes a live Infinex data point — an asset price, a yield rate, a wallet stat, a market depth, a swap quote, a portfolio delta — and treats THAT as the frame's protagonist (big-numeric, Inter-bold, a single restrained brand accent, a one-line wry caption, a small thumbnail/icon) gives us the same memetic advantage: every video is a screenshot waiting to be re-shared. Build a "data card" template family before any "narrative" template family. The trap: do not steal Polymarket's actual palette or type — Poly Blue + Inter is THEIR uniform, and any Infinex piece that wears it reads as a Polymarket post. Take the structural move (data-as-poster, restraint as personality, embed-able as principle) and dress it in Infinex's identity-kit colors and type. Also: resist the temptation to add motion just because Remotion can. Polymarket's tempo lesson is that newsroom-pace + a single drawing-in line chart is enough; their videos win by NOT looking like crypto videos. Match that restraint before deciding to break it on purpose.
