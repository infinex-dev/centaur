# comms-factory — market thesis (addendum to PRODUCT-MAP)

Companion doc to [`PRODUCT-MAP.md`](PRODUCT-MAP.md). Where PRODUCT-MAP describes the artefact, this describes the market reality, the actual differentiation thesis, and the productization frontier — written after three independent market assessments came back with overlapping pessimism on the surface framing and overlapping blindness on the underlying claim.

Tiered the same way: read tier 0 in 30 seconds; tier 5 in 30 minutes.

---

## Tier 0 — Front page

Three independent commissioned reports converged on a sharp critique: **don't productize "voice infrastructure for AI agents."** That category is consolidating around Writer.com + Acrolinx (brand-voice governance) and Sierra + Decagon (agent platforms). Soul Machines raised NZ$225M shipping premium humanized character and went to receivership in Feb 2026. The reports are right about this slice.

But all three reports missed the actual claim. They evaluated **"structured agent persona"** against Big-Five sliders, MBTI scores, `character.json` adjective lists, and prompt-engineered backstories — none of which is what Laban/Mirodan does. None of them mentioned Laban once.

The actual category Laban-as-applied-to-brands competes in is **verbal identity consultancy** — Lippincott, Interbrand, Landor, Wolff Olins, Pentagram, R/GA. A $1–3B global premium-tier annual market that the reports didn't engage because the consultancies don't publish their methods, so no public corpus exists to evaluate against. AI agents are one downstream consumer of a CharacterSpec; copywriters, comms ops, social handles, support reps, internal evangelism are the others. **The headline is a comprehensive person / communicator, not an agent persona.**

On the verbal-identity sub-deliverable, Laban beats Lippincott on every measurable axis — specificity, falsifiability, auditability, drift detection, cross-surface composition, cost, time-to-spec. That's the wedge. Productize it correctly and the realistic 5–7 year ceiling is **$50–200M ARR** across consulting + workshops + tooling + multi-agent layer. Very good business; not a unicorn unless the agent layer accelerates.

## Tier 1 — The category, restated (5 min)

PRODUCT-MAP framed Surface 3 as "voice infrastructure for the agent economy." That framing puts the product on a competitive map dominated by Sierra ($15.8B), Decagon ($4.5B), Writer.com, Acrolinx. We lose that fight even if Laban is the better framework, because the buyer doesn't purchase a separate "character spec" line item — it bundles into the agent platform or the brand-voice governance suite they already bought.

Restate the headline:

> **Laban produces a falsifiable, comprehensive verbal identity — a complete "person" — that downstream consumers (writers, agents, support reps, social handles) execute against. It's what Lippincott sells, mechanically.**

That places the product in the **verbal identity consultancy** category, not the AI infra category. The competitors become Lippincott, Interbrand, Landor, Wolff Olins, Verbal Identity Ltd., COLLINS — firms that today ship 40–100 page strategy decks containing adjectives + tone spectrum + lexicon + sample copy, qualitatively, in 6–12 weeks, at $250K–$1M per engagement. Mechanical falsifiability is the structural improvement none of them ship, by choice (their clients prefer flexibility; they prefer repeat engagements).

The three reports could not evaluate this category because the consultancies don't publish their methodologies — there is no public corpus to compare against. That's both the opportunity (no incumbent has a falsifiable framework) and the diagnostic limit of the reports (they keep collapsing the pitch onto Writer/Acrolinx, which is the wrong shelf).

## Tier 2 — What the three reports converged on (the real critique to engage)

Report A (Obsidian — *The Market and Technical Landscape*), Report B (Compass — *An Honest Market Assessment*), Report C (Deep-Research — *Market need for mechanically auditable infrastructure*). Independently commissioned, independent corpora, independent conclusions. Three things they agree on, in descending importance:

**1. The "good enough" paradigm is real.** LLM-as-judge + sample-extracted style profiles + Acrolinx-style content scoring eats 80% of what enterprise buyers actually purchase voice tooling for. Single-agent voice is not the problem the market is begging to solve. Without a clear answer to "for whom is good enough not good enough?" the framework is over-engineered.

**2. The SaaS-vs-consulting trap is structural.** Translating "warm but authoritative" → a Laban placement requires either a consultant (not scalable) or a non-engineer operator running a deterministic skill (the bet). Pulsely's collapse + the MIT NANDA "95% of GenAI pilots fail on integration not algorithm" stat is the realistic outcome of trying to SaaS-ify domain-knowledge bridging.

**3. The buyer doesn't exist as a separate line item.** Voice/persona bundles into agent platforms or brand-governance stacks. Soul Machines is the cautionary tale — deep character infrastructure with NZ$225M raised, receivership Feb 2026, because commodity LLMs were close-enough.

Three points the addendum has to answer directly, not gloss.

## Tier 3 — What the three reports missed (where the wedge actually lives)

**1. None of them have Mirodan/Laban in their corpus.** They catalogue every adjacent framework — PERSONAGE, OCC, BDI, IBSEN, Dramatron, Propp, Big Five, MBTI, persona vectors, Biber, NeuroBiber, M2HRI, MAJ-EVAL — but Laban/Mirodan is absent. That's either genuinely novel IP or invisible to public corpora. Either way it means: when Report B says *"no mechanically falsifiable framework exists,"* the reports cannot evaluate whether comms-factory is that framework.

**2. They flatten "structured character" to schemas.** Big-Five-with-sliders, adjective lists, `character.json`. They miss that Laban is *deductive*: one placement (Inner Attitude × Aspect × Stress) mechanically derives the X-diagram, the 4 corner drives, the action axis, the tempo rotation, the motor pairs, the literary anchor, and the off-spec drives. It's not a list of traits — it's a tree where one decision propagates constraints. That property is precisely what the reports say no public framework has. They missed it because they searched for it as a list.

**3. Nigel-in-production is invisible.** The reports cannot find any production application of theatrical-character frameworks to AI agents because Nigel's source is private. They keep concluding *"ingredients exist but scattered, no production deployment."* That's wrong — the production deployment exists. It just isn't publicly indexable.

**4. The Phantom audit is its own category.** Forensic verbal-identity audit on a competitor brand's full corpus, producing a structural diagnosis (placement match vs intent, off-spec drift, cross-surface coherence). The reports compare this against Acrolinx-style editorial scoring; those aren't the same product. Forensic-stylometry-for-brand-coherence is a category Lippincott can't ship because they have no mechanical model to score against.

**5. The verbal identity consultancy category is missing from their evaluation entirely.** They cover Writer/Acrolinx (governance tooling), Sierra/Decagon (agent platforms), Inworld/Convai (gaming character), Hume (prosody). Lippincott / Interbrand / Landor get mentioned but as "consultancies who don't publish frameworks" — not as competitors to be displaced. That's the actual lane.

## Tier 4 — Laban vs Lippincott (the actual differentiation pitch)

Lippincott (and the whole peer group — Interbrand, Landor, Wolff Olins, Pentagram, R/GA, COLLINS) ship verbal identity as a 6–12 week bespoke engagement. Deliverable: 40–100 page strategy document containing brand purpose, voice principles (3–5 adjectives), tone spectrum, messaging hierarchy, lexicon, sample copy, naming if commissioned. Price: $250K–$1M per engagement at premium tier; $50K–$250K at mid-tier verbal-identity boutique.

On the verbal-identity sub-deliverable specifically, Laban beats Lippincott on every measurable axis:

| Axis | Lippincott | Laban |
|---|---|---|
| **Spec primitive** | 3–5 adjectives ("confident, warm, irreverent") | One cell on a 24-cell map (Inner × Aspect × Stress → Drive Axis) |
| **Ambiguity** | High — adjectives alias across 100+ brands; copywriters interpret variably | Low — placement is a discrete tuple with factor-coherence rules |
| **Constraint generation** | N independent qualitative judgments per engagement | One placement deductively generates the X-diagram, action axis, tempo rotation, motor pairs, literary anchor, off-spec drives |
| **Off-spec specification** | "We lean against urgency" — descriptive, not testable | "Passion drive is off-spec; here are the lexical/syntactic activators" — testable |
| **Auditability of shipped copy** | None — engagement ends at the deliverable | Classifier reads N samples + reports placement-actually-hit vs placement-on-spec |
| **Cross-surface coherence** | Qualitative judgment by brand director | Placements compose on a shared map — fleet-level reasoning is mechanical |
| **Vocabulary depth** | Sentence-level ("use active voice") | Paragraph-level structural rhythm via Working Action motor pairs |
| **Drift detection** | Annual brand health audit (qualitative) | Validator runs on every shipped piece; drift surfaces in days |
| **Framework grounding** | Sui generis per engagement | 70-year synthesis of theatre character work; 24 cells are a tested behavioural grammar |
| **Time-to-spec** | 6–12 weeks | 1–2 weeks with the operator skill |
| **Cost** | $250K–$1M premium tier | Order of magnitude lower |

**What Lippincott has that Laban doesn't** (the honest gap):

- C-suite procurement credibility. "We hired Lippincott" is a board-level status signal that Laban can't match in year 1–2.
- Visual identity, naming, brand architecture, broader strategy — Laban is verbal-grain only.
- Workshops, internal evangelism, change-management infrastructure.
- 80 years of relationship capital with CMOs.

**Two routes to entry:**

a) **Compete with verbal-identity-specialist boutiques** (Verbal Identity Ltd., the Chris West tradition, mid-tier voice studios) for whom voice IS the whole service. Win on rigor + speed + auditability.

b) **License the methodology to Lippincott-tier firms** as the rigor layer behind their existing offering. They keep the C-suite relationship; you take 15–25% of the verbal sub-engagement as a methodology fee. Strategyzer's Business Model Canvas licensing model.

Both are real. (a) builds the brand; (b) scales without sales-cycle pain.

What this is *not*: a $1B+ horizontal SaaS. The reports were right about that. The path to that outcome would require category creation at a pace the operator-bandwidth + the rigor problem makes unrealistic.

## Tier 6 — The operator / rigor productization frontier

The single biggest unlock between current state and the mid-market layer ($20–100M ARR slice) is **making the spec authoring skill drivable by a non-Laban-literate operator**.

Current state: Opus running the inside-out interview skill regularly trips on factor coherence (Stress can't be Weight; Aspect must be available given the Inner's factor pair; certain placements are illegal). Recovery requires a Laban-literate human in the loop to catch misroutes. That gates scalability — you become the operator forever, which is consulting in SaaS clothing, exactly the trap the reports warn about.

**What needs to ship for the skill to be operator-optional:**

**1. Deterministic interview flow.** Don't let the model improvise question order. Hard-coded sequence: sample-recognition first → backward-derivation → OOP probes → factor-coherence verification → placement lock. The skill becomes interrogator-like, not assistant-like.

**2. Sample-recognition as the primary input.** Already validated as the methodology that works (see `methodology-inside-out-interview.md`). Codify it: build a snippet library of 24 × 3 = 72 anchor samples (3 per cell). Run the interview through forced-choice rounds: *"which of these 6 character snippets sounds most like your brand?"* The operator's gut about samples beats their articulation of abstract preferences.

**3. OOP diagnostics built in.** After every tentative placement, the skill auto-probes the four adjacent cells: *"you locked Stable+Penetrating; let me also test Stable+Enclosing and Adream+Penetrating against these same samples to verify."* If an adjacent cell fits better, the placement drifted and gets re-derived. From `methodology-out-of-placement-diagnostic.md`.

**4. Factor coherence as a hard validator.** Reject illegal Aspect/Stress/Inner combinations at input time with explanation. Don't make the operator memorize the rules; encode them as gates.

**5. Cold re-derivation pass.** After placement lock, run a fresh-context model over the same evidence and check whether it arrives at the same cell. Disagreement → re-interview before shipping. The skill's own self-audit.

Once those five are in, the skill becomes the operator. Then the workshop product unlocks:

> **One day cohort. Twelve attendees. Leave with a draft CharacterSpec for your own brand, validated by the system, plus the methodology to maintain it.**

Workshops are pure margin, recurring, scale linearly with curriculum maturity, and convert attendees into either tooling customers or licensing prospects. At $5–15K/seat × 12 seats × N cohorts/year, this is the cleanest path to mid-six-figure ARR off the existing repo state without needing to win the agent-economy fight.

## Tier 7 — Sequenced 60-day moves (under Infinex-invisibility constraint)

Hard constraint: nothing about Infinex's internal voice work, current placement, or shipped applications becomes externally visible. The case studies have to come from elsewhere.

**Days 0–10 — Finish the Phantom audit as the public artefact.**

Output: a publishable forensic verbal-identity case study on a major Web3 brand, derived mechanically, with viz + drive analysis + off-spec inventory. *Proves: we read whole identities forensically.*

**Days 10–25 — Author one full CharacterSpec for a non-Infinex brand publicly.**
Targets in priority order: (a) friendly external design partner you can spec with permission, (b) a public/historical brand with sufficient corpus (Patagonia, Liquid Death, A24, Berkshire Hathaway letters, etc.), (c) one of the existing internal specs (`cream.ts`, `projectjin.ts`) if publishable. Artefact: inside-out interview transcript → classified corpus → locked CharacterSpec → tone-of-voice document → off-spec regex library → validator-ready prompt. End-to-end methodology, visible from start to finish. *Proves: we author whole identities mechanically, faster than Lippincott.*

**Days 25–40 — Harden the skill so a non-engineer operator can run it.**
Implement the five items in tier 6: deterministic flow, sample-recognition library, OOP diagnostics, factor-coherence gates, cold re-derivation. Cold-test on someone with zero Laban literacy. Iterate until they can complete a placement without operator intervention. *Unblocks the workshop product.*

**Days 40–55 — One commissioned external pilot.**
With the Phantom case study + the public-spec exemplar + the operator-runnable skill, approach one design partner outside Infinex for a paid or in-kind pilot. Deliverable: a locked CharacterSpec they ship copy against, with a validator they run themselves. Measure: review-cycle compression, off-spec catches/month, coherence delta against pre-existing corpus. *One real external customer with quantifiable outcome is the single artefact the reports say is missing.*

**Days 55–60 — Write the publishable thesis.**
Long-form: *Mechanically falsifiable verbal identity: what Lippincott does, made auditable.* Not a fundraising deck — a category-claim piece, with the Phantom audit + the external pilot as evidence. Sub-frame: workshops, methodology licensing, multi-agent extensions as forward roadmap. *Converts "intellectually profound but commercially perilous" into a credible category claim, without exposing Infinex.*

Sequencing is load-bearing: each step compounds the credibility of the next. Nigel stays as a one-line reference in the thesis ("there's a live trading bot using this every day") but isn't the headline.

## Tier 8 — Kill triggers (when to stop)

Adapted from Report B's framing. Watch list with explicit exits:

- **Anthropic ships a dimensional persona-spec object in the Claude Agent SDK with built-in evals** → the open-source-spec wedge collapses to commodity. Likely 12–18 months. Stop or pivot.
- **OpenAI Agents SDK ships the same** → same outcome.
- **Letta's persona memory block evolves into a dimensional schema with eval tooling** → integrate-not-compete. Build the Laban-to-Letta compiler.
- **A single open-source character spec (Eliza/Virtuals-side) reaches >50% adoption among top-50 crypto agents** → the standard is set. Integrate or pivot to multi-agent layer.
- **A top-3 CX agent platform (Sierra/Decagon/Cresta) ships a public voice-eval benchmark with vendor-neutral metrics** → market is internalizing the work. Stop.
- **Lippincott or Interbrand publicly ships a productized verbal-identity framework with mechanical evaluation** → direct head-to-head. Compete on rigor + price + speed, but the bar gets higher.
- **The Phantom audit + external pilot together fail to produce a measurable coherence delta** → the framework doesn't produce the value the pitch claims. Stop and re-examine.
- **The operator skill can't be made drivable by non-Laban-literate users within Q3 2026** → the consulting trap wins. Pivot to a higher-touch consultancy model or shelve.
